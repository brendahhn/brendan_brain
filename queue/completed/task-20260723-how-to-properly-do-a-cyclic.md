### 2026-07-24 [sonnet] — How to properly do a cyclic (physiological) sigh

**The technique, exactly [A/B]**

A cyclic sigh is a repeated *physiological sigh*. One rep:

1. **Double inhale through the nose.** Take one full, smooth inhale through the nose — then, without exhaling, add a second short, sharp "top-off" sip of air through the nose to maximally inflate the lungs. Two inhales, one exhale.
2. **Long, slow exhale through the mouth.** Exhale slowly and fully through the mouth (pursed lips / gentle sigh) until the lungs feel empty. The exhale should be **noticeably longer than the combined inhales** — this extended exhale is the active ingredient.
3. **Repeat continuously** for ~5 minutes (that is the "cyclic" part). Breathe at your own comfortable pace; there is no forced count — the only rule is exhale > inhale, every cycle.

**Why it works [B — mechanistic]**

- **Reinflating alveoli.** Under stress and shallow breathing, some alveoli (lung air sacs) collapse. The second "top-off" inhale pops them back open, restoring surface area for gas exchange. This is the same reflex the body does spontaneously every few minutes and when we cry/recover.
- **Offloading CO2.** Rising CO2 is a direct biochemical trigger of the "air hunger"/anxiety signal the brainstem monitors. The long exhale dumps CO2 efficiently, quieting that alarm.
- **Parasympathetic shift.** Extended exhales increase vagal tone: heart rate slows on each out-breath (respiratory sinus arrhythmia), tipping the autonomic balance from sympathetic ("fight/flight") toward parasympathetic ("rest"). The prolonged exhale is the single most important component for the calming effect. [A for the "longer exhale calms" direction; B for the precise vagal mechanism]

**Dose / timing [A for the 5-min daily dose; B for acute use]**

- **Daily practice:** 5 minutes/day. In the Stanford trial this beat other methods for improving mood and lowering resting respiratory rate, and the benefit *grew* over the month (cumulative, not just in-the-moment).
- **Acute / in-the-moment:** just **1–3 physiological sighs** can pull you back toward calm quickly — useful before a stressful call, mid-spike of anxiety, to fall asleep, or to reset focus. You don't need the full 5 minutes for on-the-spot relief.
- **When to use:** anytime; no special posture or equipment. Good as a pre-sleep wind-down, a pre-event nerves reset, or a daily 5-min "hygiene" practice.

**Common mistakes to avoid [B]**

- **Making the second inhale too big.** It's a short top-off, not a second full breath. Stacking two big inhales causes mild hyperventilation and defeats the purpose. Second inhale should be briefer and quieter than the first.
- **Rushing / shortening the exhale.** The exhale must be the longest, most unhurried part. If your exhale isn't clearly longer than your inhale, you're not getting the parasympathetic effect.
- **Inhaling through the mouth or exhaling through the nose.** Standard protocol is inhale nose, exhale mouth.
- **Forcing a rigid count or over-effort.** It should feel easy and passive on the exhale, not strained.

**How it compares (per the study) [A]**

In the trial, cyclic sighing (exhale-emphasized) outperformed **box breathing** (equal inhale/hold/exhale/hold) and **cyclic hyperventilation with retention** (longer inhales, shorter exhales — a Wim-Hof-style, sympathetic-leaning pattern), and also beat **mindfulness meditation**, for improving daily mood and reducing respiratory rate. Takeaway: among short breathwork practices, **the one that emphasizes the exhale wins** for calming. All breathwork arms beat meditation on these measures.
