### 2026-07-24 — Research pass `[sonnet]`

**Q1 — Is xylitol good for teeth? (with your UC in mind)**

Dental mechanism: xylitol is a 5-carbon sugar alcohol that *Streptococcus mutans* (the main
cavity bug) cannot ferment into acid; it also gets taken up by S. mutans in a futile cycle that
suppresses its growth, and chewing stimulates saliva (buffers acid, remineralizes). [B]
mechanistic — well-established.

Strength of evidence: weaker than the marketing suggests. The 2015 Cochrane review found only
*low-to-very-low quality* evidence, mostly one fluoride-toothpaste trial; insufficient to
confirm benefit from gums/lozenges/candies on their own. [A] (that the *evidence is weak* is the
strong, well-replicated finding). Later meta-analyses (2022) are more favorable and give a usable
dose signal but are lower-tier than Cochrane. [B]

Effective dose/form: benefit appears only at ~**5-10 g/day split over 3-5 exposures**, each
chewed ~5 min, ideally after meals; <3x/day (<~3.4 g/day) showed no benefit. Gum/lozenge >>
toothpaste (toothpaste contact time is too short to matter for the xylitol itself). [B]

**The gut tension (this is the part that's specific to you):** xylitol is a fermentable polyol —
the same FODMAP family flagged repeatedly in your health notebook. It's dose-dependently
laxative/gas-forming in *anyone*; the loose-stool threshold in healthy adults is often cited
around ~40-50 g/day but is much lower for sensitive/unaccustomed guts. More pointedly, a **June
2025 iScience mouse study** showed dietary fermentable polyols (sorbitol, also mannitol)
*worsened* DSS colitis via M1-macrophage/IL-1β-driven inflammation — mechanistically concerning
for IBD, though it tested sorbitol/mannitol, not xylitol, in mice, not humans. [C] for "xylitol
specifically harms a human UC colon" — that's an inference, not a demonstrated fact.

Bottom line for you: A few pieces of **xylitol gum/lozenge after meals is a reasonable, low-risk
dental habit** — the per-piece dose (~0.7-1 g) is tiny and gum's saliva boost helps regardless of
the xylitol. The dental *upside is real but modest and modestly-evidenced*; the gut *downside is
real but dose-dependent* and only bites at higher intakes or during a flare. Keep total xylitol
well under a few grams/day, favor gum over swallowed lozenges/mints, and **skip it during an
active flare**. Don't go chasing the "therapeutic" 6-10 g/day dental dose — for your gut that's
the wrong end of the dose-response curve. Provider-gated: whether to use xylitol at all during
active disease is worth a line to your GI.

**Q2 — Best morning (hot) tea for you**

Framing: on an empty stomach, high-caffeine + high-tannin teas (strong black, and to a lesser
degree green) can hit a sensitive gut harder — tannins can nag the stomach and caffeine is a gut
motility stimulant. Human trial data on tea *for UC specifically* is thin across the board. [B]

Ranked for you:
1. **Rooibos (red bush)** — caffeine-free, naturally *low-tannin* (<5%), polyphenol/aspalathin
   antioxidant load; anti-inflammatory effects shown in colitis *models*. Gentlest first-thing
   option; won't stimulate the gut. Best default morning cup. [B] mechanistic/animal, [C] for
   proven UC benefit.
2. **Ginger** — caffeine-free; gingerols/shogaols are anti-inflammatory and it's genuinely
   settling for nausea/gut. Great rotation or add-in. [B]
3. **Green tea** — the EGCG story is real (a Polyphenon-E/green-tea-extract pilot was run in UC),
   but that's concentrated *extract*, not a mug of tea, and the mug brings caffeine + tannins on
   an empty stomach. Fine if you want the ritual/caffeine — brew lighter, and ideally with/after
   food. [B]
4. **Chamomile** — calming, mild antispasmodic; weak but harmless evidence. Nice evening more
   than morning. [C]
5. **Peppermint** — helps IBS-type cramping/spasm but *relaxes the lower esophageal sphincter and
   can worsen reflux*; only if you don't get heartburn. [B]

Bottom line: make **rooibos your default morning cup** (caffeine-free, low-tannin, gut-gentle),
keep **ginger** in rotation, and treat **green tea** as the "I want caffeine" option to have
lighter and closer to food rather than on an empty stomach.
